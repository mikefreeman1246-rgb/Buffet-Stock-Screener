  BENJAMIN GRAHAM'S INTRINSIC VALUE FORMULA
  A Complete Reference Guide
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  THE FORMULA
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g) × (4.4 / Y)

  As used in this application (May 2026):

  V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g) × (4.4 / 5.56)
  V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g) × 0.7914


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  VARIABLES DEFINED
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  V   = Intrinsic Value per share (what the stock is worth)

  EPS = Trailing Twelve-Month (TTM) diluted earnings per share.
        For cyclical businesses (energy, steel, housing) Graham
        recommended using a normalized, mid-cycle EPS rather
        than the most recent figure, to avoid over- or under-
        weighting a single year's result.

  8.5 = The base Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio Graham assigned
        to a company with ZERO expected earnings growth.
        A no-growth business deserves a low multiple — 8.5x.

  g   = Expected annual EPS growth rate over the next 7–10 years,
        expressed as a whole-number percentage.
        Example: if analysts expect 12% annual growth, g = 12.
        Graham capped g at a maximum of around 20 in practice,
        as extremely high growth rates are inherently unreliable
        over a decade-long horizon.

  4.4 = The yield on AAA-rated corporate bonds at the time Graham
        published the formula (early 1960s). This was his proxy
        for the "risk-free" return an investor could expect from
        the highest-quality fixed income at that time.

  Y   = The CURRENT yield on AAA-rated corporate bonds.
        As of May 2026: Y = 5.56% (Moody's AAA index, FRED).
        The ratio 4.4/Y adjusts the formula for today's interest
        rate environment. When rates are high, 4.4/Y < 1, which
        compresses intrinsic values. When rates are low, 4.4/Y > 1,
        which expands them. This reflects the core financial truth
        that rising rates reduce the present value of future earnings.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  THE MULTIPLIER EXPLAINED
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  The term (8.5 + 2g) is Graham's P/E multiplier.

  At g = 0  →  multiplier = 8.5    (no-growth business)
  At g = 5  →  multiplier = 18.5
  At g = 10 →  multiplier = 28.5
  At g = 15 →  multiplier = 38.5
  At g = 20 →  multiplier = 48.5   (Graham's practical ceiling)

  The "+2g" component means every additional percentage point of
  expected annual growth adds 2 points to the fair P/E. This is
  a linear approximation of what the market typically pays for
  growth — not a precise mathematical derivation.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  WORKED EXAMPLE — COCA-COLA (KO)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  EPS = $2.95
  g   = 6% (expected annual growth)
  Y   = 5.56%

  Step 1: Calculate the multiplier
          8.5 + (2 × 6) = 8.5 + 12 = 20.5

  Step 2: Apply the bond yield adjustment
          4.4 / 5.56 = 0.7914

  Step 3: Calculate intrinsic value
          V = 2.95 × 20.5 × 0.7914
          V = 2.95 × 16.22
          V ≈ $47.85

  Current price: $81.35
  Premium to Graham Value: +70%
  Verdict: Overvalued by Graham's metric

  Sweet Spot (33% margin of safety): ≤ $35.89
  This means Graham would want to buy KO below ~$35.89

  THE MARGIN OF SAFETY — GRAHAM'S GOLDEN RULE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham never bought at intrinsic value. He demanded a DISCOUNT
  to protect against errors in his own estimates.

  His standard: buy only when price ≤ 66–75% of intrinsic value.
  This 25–33% discount is the "margin of safety."

  In this application, Sweet Spot = Graham Value × 0.75
  (a 25% discount / 33% margin of safety)

  Graham's reasoning:
    — EPS estimates can be wrong
    — Growth rates are inherently uncertain
    — Bond yields can change
    — Companies face unexpected adversity

  The margin of safety is the investor's buffer against all of
  these uncertainties. "The three most important words in
  investing are margin of safety." — Benjamin Graham

  GRAHAM'S VERDICT THRESHOLDS (as used in this app)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Price vs. Graham Value   →  Verdict
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  Price < 85% of Graham V  →  Undervalued   (potential buy)
  85%–110% of Graham V     →  Fair Value    (fairly priced)
  110%–135% of Graham V    →  Mild Overval. (caution)
  Price > 135% of Graham V →  Overvalued    (avoid / sell)

  WHAT THE FORMULA DOES WELL
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  ✓ Simple, fast, and requires minimal inputs
  ✓ Built-in interest rate sensitivity (the Y adjustment)
  ✓ Excellent for stable, industrial-era businesses
  ✓ Forces discipline: a high price is a high price
  ✓ Embeds the margin of safety concept mathematically
  ✓ Works well for: banks, retailers, energy, industrials,
    consumer staples with moderate growth

  KNOWN LIMITATIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  ✗ Fails for negative or near-zero EPS (result is negative)
  ✗ Assumes a LINEAR relationship between growth and value
    — reality is exponential and depends on reinvestment rates
  ✗ No concept of competitive moat or earnings durability
  ✗ Undervalues asset-light companies where D&A exceeds capex
    (reported EPS understates true cash generation)
  ✗ Growth rate "g" is the analyst's estimate — highly uncertain
  ✗ Often labels high-quality franchises as "overvalued" when
    the market is correctly pricing in a durable advantage
  ✗ Not designed for financial companies (banks, insurers) whose
    earnings are structured fundamentally differently
  ✗ Does not account for share buybacks, which amplify
    per-share earnings growth beyond the reported EPS growth rate

  HISTORICAL CONTEXT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Benjamin Graham (1894–1976) published this formula in the
  1962 edition of "Security Analysis" and refined it in later
  editions of "The Intelligent Investor."

  Graham's investing philosophy was shaped by the Great Depression.
  He sought mathematical proof that an investment was safe —
  particularly that assets or earnings justified the price even
  in a worst-case scenario. His formulas were designed to find
  a "liquidation floor" beneath which the stock was almost
  certainly cheap regardless of future prospects.

  His most famous students: Warren Buffett (Columbia, 1950),
  Irving Kahn, Walter Schloss, and Charlie Munger (indirectly).

  Buffett called Graham "the second most influential person in
  my life after my father."
