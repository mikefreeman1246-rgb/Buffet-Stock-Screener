  WARREN BUFFETT'S MODERNIZATION OF GRAHAM'S FORMULA
  The Four Key Modifications + Five Modern Additions
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."
  — Warren Buffett

  Buffett studied under Graham at Columbia (1950) and credits
  him as the most important influence on his investment
  philosophy. But over 70+ years of investing, Buffett —
  largely through the influence of Charlie Munger — evolved
  well beyond Graham's purely mathematical, asset-focused
  approach into something fundamentally different.

  The shift: Graham minimized the COST OF BEING WRONG
             by demanding a low price for anything.
             Buffett maximizes the VALUE OF BEING RIGHT
             by identifying businesses whose earnings power
             is systematically underestimated by the market.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  THE BUFFETT FORMULA (Two-Stage Owner Earnings DCF)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  As implemented in this application:

  Stage 1 (High-Growth Period, 10 years):

    If g ≠ r:
      S1 = OE × [ 1 − ((1+g)/(1+r))^10 ] / (r − g)

    If g = r (special case, L'Hôpital limit):
      S1 = 10 × OE / (1 + r)

  Stage 2 (Terminal / Perpetuity Value):

    Terminal OE = OE × (1 + g)^10
    S2 = Terminal OE × (1 + 2.5%) / [(r − 2.5%) × (1+r)^10]

  Total Intrinsic Value:
    V_buffett = S1 + S2

  Where:
    OE  = Owner Earnings per share (see Modification #1)
    g   = High-growth-period EPS growth rate (as decimal)
    r   = Discount rate (see Modification #2)
    2.5% = Terminal perpetuity growth rate (GDP-like)

  Discount rate r by moat quality:
    Wide moat companies:     r = 8.5%
    Moderate moat companies: r = 9.5%
    Narrow moat companies:   r = 10.5%
    No moat companies:       r = 11.5%

  MODIFICATION #1 — OWNER EARNINGS REPLACE REPORTED EPS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham used accounting EPS straight from the income statement.
  Buffett uses Owner Earnings, which he defined in his 1986
  shareholder letter as:

    Owner Earnings = Net Income
                   + Depreciation & Amortization
                   − Maintenance Capital Expenditures
                   − Working Capital Requirements

  WHY THIS MATTERS:

  Accounting rules require companies to "depreciate" physical
  assets over their useful lives — spreading the original cost
  across many years. This creates a non-cash expense called D&A
  that reduces reported EPS but does NOT reduce actual cash.

  For asset-light businesses (AAPL, GOOG, MCO, VRSN), D&A is
  often FAR larger than the actual cash required to maintain
  the business (maintenance capex). The difference flows
  directly to owners as cash — but it's invisible in EPS.

  EXAMPLES from Berkshire's portfolio:

  Apple (AAPL):
    — Reported EPS: $8.27
    — D&A exceeds maintenance capex significantly
    — Owner Earnings multiplier: 1.20×
    — Owner Earnings estimate: $9.92/share
    — Graham undervalues AAPL by ignoring this cash

  VeriSign (VRSN):
    — Virtually zero maintenance capex needed
    — Revenue from .com/.net domain renewals is fully automatic
    — Owner Earnings multiplier: 1.25×
    — The most capital-efficient holding in the portfolio

  Kraft Heinz (KHC):
    — Ongoing restructuring charges inflate reported earnings
    — Owner Earnings multiplier: 0.90× (adjusted DOWN)
    — Real cash generation is LESS than reported EPS
    — Graham's formula overstates KHC's value

  Rule of thumb:
    Asset-light businesses (tech, financial, brand):  OE > EPS
    Capital-heavy businesses (energy, manufacturing): OE ≈ EPS
    Businesses with restructuring charges:            OE < EPS

  MODIFICATION #2 — THE MOAT PREMIUM REPLACES A FIXED RATE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham used the same bond-yield adjustment for every company.
  Buffett uses a VARIABLE discount rate that reflects the
  certainty and durability of the competitive advantage (moat).

  "The key to investing is determining the competitive
  advantage of any given company and, above all, the durability
  of that advantage." — Buffett, 1999 Fortune interview

  MOAT TYPES AND THEIR DISCOUNT RATES:

  Wide Moat (r = 8.5%)
    Examples: AAPL, AXP, KO, GOOGL, MCO, VRSN, CB
    Characteristics:
    — Pricing power that does not erode under competition
    — Customer switching costs are high
    — Network effects or regulatory barriers to entry
    — Brand premium that persists across economic cycles
    — Buffett will hold these FOREVER at the right price

  Moderate Moat (r = 9.5%)
    Examples: BAC, DVA, KR, COF, STZ, NVR
    Characteristics:
    — Competitive advantage is real but faces some erosion risk
    — Market position is strong but not impenetrable
    — Some dependency on external factors (rates, demographics)

  Narrow Moat (r = 10.5%)
    Examples: CVX, OXY, DAL, KHC, LEN, NUE, ALLY
    Characteristics:
    — Advantage exists but could be disrupted within a decade
    — Commodity-linked earnings, regulatory risk, or secular trends
    — Requires more margin of safety to compensate

  No Moat (r = 11.5%)
    Examples: M (Macy's), LPX
    Characteristics:
    — Competing purely on price or cost
    — No durable advantage; earnings are highly uncertain
    — Graham-style asset value protection is the primary case

  THE LOGIC: A wide-moat business's future earnings are more
  CERTAIN. When you discount more certain cash flows, you use
  a lower discount rate, which produces a higher present value.
  This is mathematically identical to saying: "I'll pay more
  for predictable earnings than unpredictable ones."

  This is the single most important departure from Graham.
  Graham treated all businesses as equally uncertain — Buffett
  does not. He pays a premium for certainty.

  MODIFICATION #3 — TWO-STAGE DCF REPLACES THE STATIC MULTIPLIER
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham's (8.5 + 2g) is a STATIC multiplier — it treats
  all years of growth as identical. Buffett replaced this with
  a proper discounted cash flow model that separates:

  STAGE 1: A high-growth period of 10 years
    — Uses the analyst consensus growth rate
    — Each year's owner earnings are discounted back to today
    — Captures the explicit value of near-term growth

  STAGE 2: A terminal value after year 10
    — Assumes the business grows at GDP pace forever (2.5%)
    — This "perpetuity" value often makes up 50–70% of the
      total intrinsic value calculation
    — Highly sensitive to the discount rate chosen

  WHY TWO STAGES MATTER:
  A business growing at 20% for 10 years and then slowing to
  2.5% forever is FUNDAMENTALLY different from a business
  already growing at 2.5%. Graham's formula would produce
  wildly different results depending on where in the growth
  cycle a company happens to be measured.

  The two-stage model handles this naturally, because it
  explicitly models the high-growth window separately from
  the long-term steady state.

  SENSITIVITY NOTE:
  The terminal value is extremely sensitive to the spread
  between discount rate (r) and terminal growth rate (2.5%).
  A 1% change in r can move intrinsic value by 30–50% for
  wide-moat businesses. This is why Buffett rarely discusses
  precise intrinsic value figures — the precision is false.

  "I'd rather be vaguely right than precisely wrong."
  — Buffett (attributing to Keynes)

  MODIFICATION #4 — SPECIAL MODELS FOR FINANCIAL COMPANIES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham's formula was designed for industrial manufacturers.
  It completely fails for banks, insurance companies, and other
  financial businesses because their earnings are structured
  differently.

  A. BANKS AND CREDIT COMPANIES (BAC, AXP, COF, ALLY)

    Graham approach: fails. Banks "borrow short and lend long."
    Their balance sheet IS their business. Reported EPS is
    highly sensitive to loan loss provisions and credit cycles.

    Buffett's approach:
    1. Value banks on MID-CYCLE Return on Equity (ROE)
       — What does the bank earn in an AVERAGE credit environment?
       — Ignore peak earnings (credit boom) and trough losses
       — BAC mid-cycle ROE: ~11–13%
       — American Express ROE: 30%+ (exceptional, wide-moat)

    2. Price-to-Book as an anchor
       — A bank earning 15% ROE on book should trade above book
       — A bank earning 8% ROE should trade at or below book
       — Justified P/B = (ROE − g) / (r − g)

    3. Net Interest Margin sensitivity
       — When interest rates rise, bank earnings often improve
       — Buffett's BAC position benefits from elevated rates

    WHY AXP IS DIFFERENT FROM A BANK:
    American Express earns 30%+ ROE because it is a NETWORK
    business, not a traditional bank. The AmEx brand commands
    premium merchant fees AND premium cardholder fees. It is
    a closed-loop network — it controls both sides of every
    transaction. This is a moat, not just leverage.

  B. INSURANCE COMPANIES (CB — Chubb)

    Buffett's unique framework — the Float Model:

    Insurance float = premiums collected but not yet paid out
    as claims. This money sits with the insurer, earning
    investment returns, at essentially zero cost IF the
    insurer prices policies correctly.

    KEY METRIC: Combined Ratio
      = (Claims Paid + Operating Expenses) / Premiums Earned

      Combined Ratio < 100: Insurer earns money AND gets
                            free use of policyholder funds
      Combined Ratio > 100: Insurer loses money underwriting
                            but still has the float to invest

    Chubb's combined ratio: approximately 87%
    This means Chubb earns 13 cents of profit for every
    dollar of premium BEFORE counting investment returns.
    Its float is, in effect, "negative-cost capital" —
    Chubb is paid to hold investable assets.

    This is why Berkshire's insurance operations (GEICO,
    General Re, Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance) are the
    financial engine of the entire conglomerate. Buffett
    has called float the most important concept in his
    business career.

  C. NORMALIZED EARNINGS FOR CYCLICALS (CVX, OXY, NUE)

    For commodity-linked businesses, reported EPS in any
    given year is almost meaningless. In a commodity boom,
    EPS is high. In a bust, it may be negative.

    Buffett's approach:
    — Use a mid-cycle commodity price (e.g., $70/barrel oil)
    — Calculate what EPS would be at that normalized price
    — Value the business on that normalized earnings power

    This is why in this application:
    — CVX uses normalized EPS of $11 (vs. actual ~$6 TTM)
    — OXY EPS normalized for mid-cycle oil prices
    — NUE (Nucor) EPS normalized for mid-cycle steel prices

    Buffett's OXY investment is also informed by:
    — Warrant purchases in the $50s = floor conviction price
    — Carbon capture optionality (future value not in EPS)
    — Chemical operations that reduce oil price sensitivity

  MODERN ADDITION #1 — BUYBACK YIELD AMPLIFICATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham had no mechanism for share buybacks. In the 1950s,
  buybacks were relatively rare. Today they are the primary
  way most large-cap US companies return capital.

  When a company buys back shares, the SHARE COUNT falls.
  The same total earnings now divide among fewer shares, so
  EPS grows AUTOMATICALLY — with zero improvement in the
  underlying business.

  Apple (AAPL) example:
    — Repurchases approximately 3–4% of shares outstanding per year
    — $95+ billion in buybacks in fiscal 2023 alone
    — Even if Apple's total profits don't grow, EPS grows 3–4%
      from share count reduction alone
    — This growth is MORE CERTAIN than organic business growth

  Buffett explicitly factors this into his Apple thesis:
  "Apple is an even better business than I originally thought
  because of its ability to return capital at such scale."

  In this application, Apple uses an Owner Earnings multiplier
  of 1.20× AND a g (growth rate) of 10%, which partly reflects
  buyback-driven EPS compounding above reported earnings growth.

  MODERN ADDITION #2 — THE WARRANT SIGNAL (OXY)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham's formula produces a single point estimate for
  intrinsic value. Buffett sometimes reveals his floor
  conviction price through the STRUCTURE of his investment.

  Berkshire's OXY warrants:
    — Acquired the right to buy 84 million OXY shares at $59.62
    — These warrants only have value if OXY stays above $59.62
    — By holding these warrants, Buffett is publicly signaling
      that he believes OXY has durable value well above $59.62

  This is a form of revealed preference — his investment
  structure discloses information his public statements do not.

  Graham's formula suggests OXY is undervalued at $59.72.
  The warrant signal reinforces this with a concrete floor.

  MODERN ADDITION #3 — RECURRING REVENUE PREMIUM
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham valued all earnings streams identically. Buffett
  pays more for earnings that are structurally recurring and
  non-discretionary.

  DaVita (DVA): ~74% of US dialysis market
    — Dialysis is non-discretionary: miss it, you die
    — Revenue is primarily Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement
    — Federal government backstops the revenue stream
    — This certainty justifies a lower discount rate (9.5%)
      than the business model alone would suggest

  Coca-Cola (KO):
    — Sells 2 billion servings per day across 200+ countries
    — The beverage habit (morning Coke, afternoon Coke) is
      as close to a recurring subscription as a consumer good
      can be without being a subscription
    — Cost basis of $3.25/share in 1988 now yields ~60%+
      annually on original investment — a "perpetual bond"
      with a growing coupon

  The lower the discount rate applied to recurring revenue,
  the more valuable it is vs. equivalent one-time revenue.

  MODERN ADDITION #4 — ASSET-LIGHT ROIC PREMIUM
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Graham focused on ASSET VALUE as a protection floor.
  Buffett, via Munger, learned to prefer businesses with
  HIGH RETURN ON INVESTED CAPITAL (ROIC) — especially those
  that need little capital to generate high returns.

  The ideal Buffett business:
    — Grows earnings rapidly
    — Requires little or no additional capital to grow
    — Can return excess cash to shareholders via dividends
      or buybacks each year

  VeriSign (VRSN) is the extreme example in this portfolio:
    — Government-granted monopoly on .com and .net registrations
    — Price increases are pre-approved and automatic
    — Zero meaningful capital expenditure required
    — Owner Earnings multiplier: 1.25× (highest in portfolio)
    — ROIC is effectively infinite (no capital base required)

  NVR (homebuilder) takes an unusual approach:
    — Never buys land outright — uses OPTIONS to purchase lots
    — Only exercises the option when a house is pre-sold
    — This means NVR carries almost no land inventory risk
    — ROIC of 30%+ vs. peers at 10–15%
    — Graham would see a small company. Buffett sees a
      capital-efficient compounder.

  MODERN ADDITION #5 — SUM-OF-PARTS FOR COMPLEX STRUCTURES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Some holdings cannot be valued with a single earnings stream.
  For these, Buffett uses an asset or sum-of-parts approach.

  Liberty Live (LLIVE):
    — Holds stakes in live entertainment venues, Ticketmaster
      interest, Formula 1 rights, and other assets
    — No single earnings stream captures the NAV
    — Value = sum of individual asset values

  Macy's (M):
    — Classic Graham play: real estate value exceeds market cap
    — Buffett (and activist investors) value the Macy's Herald
      Square building and other owned properties
    — Earnings from retail are secondary to asset liquidation value
    — This IS Graham-style investing — the only pure example
      in the current portfolio

  BUFFETT VERDICT THRESHOLDS (as used in this app)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  The Buffett ratio = Price / Buffett Intrinsic Value
  A moat bonus is added: Wide +5%, Moderate +2%, other +0%

  Adjusted ratio    →  Verdict
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  ≥ 1.50            →  ★ Strong Buy
  1.20 – 1.49       →  ▲ Buy
  0.92 – 1.19       →  ◆ Hold
  0.78 – 0.91       →  ▼ Trim
  < 0.78            →  ✕ Pass

  NOTE: Buffett rarely sells even at "Trim" or "Pass" for
  wide-moat positions he has held for decades. The cost basis
  matters significantly — selling triggers capital gains tax,
  which is itself a hidden cost. His standard: "Our favorite
  holding period is forever."

  THE FUNDAMENTAL PHILOSOPHICAL SHIFT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  GRAHAM: Buy cigar butts. One last puff left.
    — Look for beaten-down companies trading below asset value
    — Any business can be a buy at a low enough price
    — Diversify widely because individual picks may fail
    — Sell when the discount to intrinsic value closes

  BUFFETT / MUNGER: Buy great businesses at fair prices.
    — Identify businesses with durable competitive advantages
    — Willingness to pay a fair or even slight premium for quality
    — Concentrate in your best ideas
    — Never sell if the business remains excellent
    — Time is the friend of a wonderful business and the
      enemy of a mediocre one

  Buffett has said:
  "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price
  than a fair company at a wonderful price."

  This is the complete inversion of Graham's approach — and
  it is the single sentence that best explains how Buffett
  became the greatest investor in history.

  Use GRAHAM primarily for:
    Stable industrials, retailers, commodity producers,
    moderately-growing consumer companies, asset plays

  Use BUFFETT primarily for:
    High-quality franchises, asset-light compounders,
    financial services with high ROE, insurance float plays,
    any business where durability of earnings is the key question

  Use BOTH and compare:
    If Graham says undervalued AND Buffett says Strong Buy →
    Maximum conviction entry (rare combination)

    If Graham says overvalued but Buffett says Buy →
    The moat justifies the premium; Graham misses the quality

    If both say overvalued →
    Strong signal to wait for a better price
